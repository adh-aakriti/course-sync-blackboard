vast amounts of information in weights, which:

Computations are mostly automatic:
• Neural networks operate via automatic activation of layers
• Don’t “choose” which memories to activate
• don’t have introspection or access to their hidden states

The neuron:
• Simple cells: Respond to very specific featurese.g., a line at a
particular orientation in a particular location of the visual field.
• Complex cells: Respond to more abstract features e.g., oriented
lines anywhere within a region, or movement in a direction.
• Hierarchical organization: The visual cortex works in layers:
simple cells detect edges, complex cells detect oriented edges &
movement) & higher-level areas detect shapes, objects, faces…
Neurons and Artificial neurons: Similarities
The first layer learns edge detectors, like simple cells
They respond to features like: vertical, horizontal, diagonal edges...
As you go deeper layers respond to more abstract patterns
Combine simple edges into: corners, textures, object parts, whole objects
hierarchical strategy: low-level features first & high-level meaning later
Heavily reuse the same filters across space (this is why NNs were
originally seen as bio-inspired networks).
Biological neurons connect mostly to nearby neurons → Convolutional
NNs use local receptive fields.
Neurons and Artificial neurons: Differences
While the inspiration is strong, ANNs are not literal models of the
cortex:
neurons in the brain are far more complex
biological vision uses massive feedback loops (NNs mostly don’t)
real neurons adapt dynamically; NN weights are fixed after training
the cortex is nonlinear in far richer ways

These used to be considered AI:
• Playing Checkers and Chess (at non-elite levels): Game-playing was the benchmark for
intelligence (e.g., early chess programs). Now: Only superhuman performance is considered AI-
worthy; basic play is trivial.
Shift point: After Deep Blue (1997), chess engines became “just algorithms.”
• Search and Information Retrieval: Early search engines were seen as exhibiting intelligence—
finding relevant information from huge datasets.
Shift point: Web-scale search in the late 1990s–2000s.
• Spell Checking & Grammar Correction: Detecting spelling or grammatical mistakes was viewed
as linguistic intelligence.
Shift point: 1980s–1990s word processors.
• Optical Character Recognition (OCR): Teaching a machine to “read” printed text was
considered a major AI milestone.
Shift point: 1990s–2000s mass digitization.
These used to be considered AI:
• Face Detection (not recognition): Detecting a human face in an image felt highly intelligent.
Shift point: Early 2000s (e.g., digital cameras with face boxes).
• Speech-to-Text (Basic Dictation): Converting spoken language into text was a core AI challenge.
Shift point: 2010s with reliable mobile dictation.
• Recommendation Systems (Simple Collaborative Filtering): Suggesting movies or products seemed AI-
like. Now: Basic personalization; only advanced models draw attention.
Shift point: Late 2000s.
• Industrial Robotics (Fixed Tasks): Robots assembling cars were described as “intelligent machines.”
Shift point: When their lack of adaptability became obvious.
• Generative AI
Programs that could generate text and images based on a prompt were
considered to be close to AGI.
Shift point: When their lack of adaptability becomes obvious???

How can all this help us learn?
● What we just saw showed that structured inputs (coordinated in time) lead to
strengthening (Long Term Potentiation) of connections
● And that "noisy" uncoordinated activity patterns lead to weakening (Long Term
Depression)
● So our brain imposes a temporal filter on its own activity patterns, as a way of
selecting those that lead to an increase in coordination of firing across synaptic
connections.
● One way to impose this is through interactions with the environment!
Source: L. White (Duke, coursera.org)
How can all this help us learn?
● In a developing brain, sensory experience might be one source of structure
that favor the potentiation of synaptic connections;
● whereas the noise (irrelevant structure) may lead to the "pruning" of synaptic
connections that are not functional.

● One of the first people to think about the plastic nature of the brain was
Canadian psychologist Donald Hebb (1904-1985).
● In The Organization of Behavior (1949), he proposed:
○ coordinated activity of a presynaptic terminal and a postsynaptic cell
would strengthen the synaptic connection between them
○ conversely, uncoordinated activity between synaptic partners would
weaken their synaptic connections
○ “neurons that fire together, wire together”
○ "out of sync, lose the link"
● experimentally proven to be informative in understanding the cellular
mechanisms of synaptic change only many years later, in the 70s and 80s
